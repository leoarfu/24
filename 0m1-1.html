Why LEO Now

MODULE 1
FOUNDATIONS

This lesson answers one question: why did low-Earth-orbit broadband become viable in the late 2010s, when the same idea was attempted and failed in the 1990s? The answer is a convergence — three industrial enablers on the supply side meeting three durable sources of demand on the pull side, at roughly the same moment. Keeping that convergence in view is the point; the specific figures change year to year, the structure does not. This is the landscape view: the orbital regimes are compared in the next lesson, the latency physics worked through in the one after, the commercial model and spectrum in their own lessons, and the detailed failure post-mortems in the case-studies module. Here we stay on "why now."

1. The Question: Why Now, and Not in the 1990s?

Low-Earth-orbit broadband is not a new idea. Several ventures attempted satellite constellations for voice and data in the 1990s — Iridium, Teledesic, and Globalstar among them — and all failed commercially, by bankruptcy or cancellation, around the turn of the century. The comparative post-mortem of those failures is covered in the case-studies module; what matters here is the shared cause.

The 1990s ventures failed for the same structural reason: satellites were expensive to build, launch was expensive, and user terminals were expensive. Those costs forced enormous break-even subscriber bases, and the ventures ran out of capital before reaching them. The missing ingredients were industrial, not conceptual. The concept was sound and ahead of its time; the cost base could not support it.

So "why now" resolves into two questions. What changed on the supply side to collapse those costs, and what demand emerged large enough to justify the capital? The rest of the lesson answers both.

THE CONSTRAINT WAS INDUSTRIAL, NOT CONCEPTUAL The 1990s did not get the physics or the architecture wrong. They got the cost base wrong — or rather, the cost base of their era made the idea impossible. The orbit was always available. What changed was the cost of using it.

2. Enabler 1 — The Smartphone Supply Chain

The first enabler is the global smartphone industry, which had nothing to do with space and changed it anyway. Smartphone volumes grew from essentially zero in 2007 to well over a billion units per year by the mid-2010s. Meeting that demand drove hundreds of billions of dollars of investment into semiconductors, MEMS sensors, radio-frequency components, and solar cells — and those are the same building blocks a small satellite uses.

A satellite attitude sensor that once cost tens of thousands of dollars as bespoke aerospace hardware became a few-dollar MEMS part from a consumer supply chain. Commodity GPS receivers handle orbit determination. Commercial-off-the-shelf processors and radiation-tolerant FPGAs survive the LEO environment at acceptable risk, helped by the fact that LEO below about 1,200 km sits beneath the worst of the radiation belts and is far gentler than GEO. The consequence is that satellite manufacturing cost fell by orders of magnitude for spacecraft built to operate at LEO altitudes.

3. Enabler 2 — Reusable Launch

The second enabler is reusable launch, which collapsed the single largest variable in constellation economics. The first successful landing of a Falcon 9 first stage in December 2015 turned reusable launch from a theory into a demonstrated practice. By reflying the same booster many times, the amortised cost per kilogram to orbit fell sharply.

The cost trajectory is the headline. The Space Shuttle era ran on the order of tens of thousands of dollars per kilogram to LEO; an expendable Falcon 9 brought that to roughly ten thousand; a reused Falcon 9 to a few thousand; and Starship, if it meets its aspirations, targets a figure low enough to change the question entirely. For an operator launching its own constellation, the effective cost can be lower still, because it pays the marginal cost of a flight rather than a market price — a structural advantage a competitor buying launch on the open market cannot match. Cadence matters alongside cost: deploying and replenishing a constellation of thousands of satellites requires launching hundreds per year, which reusable vehicles make possible.

~$10k$/kg, Falcon 9 expendable
~$2.7k$/kg, Falcon 9 reusable
<$100$/kg, Starship aspirational

4. Enabler 3 — Flat-Panel Phased-Array Terminals

The third enabler is the one most often overlooked, because it is on the user's side rather than the operator's. A constellation only generates revenue if users can afford and install a terminal, and a LEO terminal has a far harder job than a GEO one. A GEO dish points once at a fixed spot and never moves. A LEO terminal must track satellites crossing the sky in minutes, handing off from one to the next continuously.

Mechanical tracking antennas can do this, and maritime and aviation used them for years, but they are bulky, fragile, power-hungry, and expensive — impossible at consumer scale. The solution is the electronically steered phased array, which steers its beam by adjusting the phase across many small elements, with no moving parts and microsecond response. The barrier was never feasibility but cost: the semiconductor content was prohibitive until the mid-2010s, when 5G millimetre-wave silicon, defence investment in electronically scanned radar, and the general march of RF integration brought it within reach. The result was a flat terminal cheap enough to put on a roof — early consumer units around a thousand-plus dollars to manufacture, with the clear target of falling below a few hundred.

COVERED IN DEPTH LATER The physics of phased arrays is treated in the RF module, and terminal technology in depth in the user-terminals module. The point here is only that the terminal stopped being the thing that kills the business.

5. The Synthesis — None of the Three Was Sufficient Alone

The three enablers matter only together. Cheap satellites without cheap launch leave a constellation no one can afford to deploy. Cheap launch without cheap terminals leaves a system with no users. Cheap terminals with nothing overhead serve no one. The 1990s had none of the three; the late 2010s had all of them at once. The convergence, roughly between 2018 and 2022, is what made the current generation possible — and it is why "why now" is a better question than "why LEO," because the orbit was always physically available. What changed was the cost of using it.

THE CONVERGENCE Three independent industrial shifts — consumer semiconductors, reusable launch, and phased-array silicon — lined up around 2018–2022. No one of them was aimed at LEO broadband. Their coincidence is the answer to "why now."

6. The Demand Side — Three Market Pillars

Lower costs are necessary but not sufficient. A mega-constellation needs billions in capital before meaningful revenue, and capital at that scale only moves when the addressable market is large, durable, and underserved. Three distinct demand pillars each clear that bar on their own.

The first is the unconnected population. Around 2.9 billion people lacked internet access as of 2020, concentrated where terrestrial fibre and mobile are uneconomical because cost per subscriber rises with low density and difficult terrain. Satellite is the one technology whose capital cost is independent of subscriber density. The per-user economics are hard — low density and low income constrain revenue — but governments and development bodies subsidise connectivity and provide anchor-tenant contracts that change the revenue model.

The second is mobility: aviation and maritime. These users are captive, willing to pay a premium, and contracted through businesses rather than individuals. In-flight connectivity was a multi-billion-dollar annual market by 2020 and growing, served by GEO at throughputs inadequate for a full aircraft of passengers expecting streaming video. Maritime is larger still by revenue. The aviation case is taken further in the user-terminals and applications modules; the landscape point is that mobility is a high-value, business-contracted demand pillar that LEO serves better than GEO.

The third is government and defence. Ukraine's operational use of Starlink from February 2022 reshaped procurement thinking across many governments, demonstrating resilient, low-latency, broadband connectivity in a contested environment. Government revenue is structurally valuable: contracts are long, pricing is less competitive than consumer markets, and anchor tenancy de-risks the early scaling phase.

7. Why GEO Could Not Simply Answer

A fair objection is that GEO operators could have responded rather than ceding the ground. They tried, and the limits are instructive. GEO's latency is a consequence of distance and the finite speed of light, not an engineering shortfall, so it cannot be designed away — the round trip is roughly 600 ms regardless of how good the hardware is. The latency math is worked through fully in The Physics of Latency; the landscape point is that it is fixed by physics.

GEO's actual response was to add capacity through high-throughput satellites. But bandwidth and latency are independent. A high-capacity GEO satellite still delivers its capacity at 600 ms, and the internet was moving toward latency-sensitive uses — video calls, cloud gaming, interactive applications — for which more bandwidth at high latency does not help. Where the two orbits win commercially, and why, is the subject of GEO vs LEO: The Commercial Delta.

8. The Takeaway

The honest one-line answer to "why LEO now" is: three industrial enablers — cheap satellites, cheap reusable launch, and cheap flat-panel terminals — converged around 2020 with three durable demand pillars — the unconnected, mobility, and government. The specific numbers in this lesson will be out of date before long; the constellation sizes, prices, and market figures all move. The framework does not. When you evaluate any new entrant or any claim that "this time is different," ask the same two questions: have the supply-side costs closed, and is there demand large and durable enough to justify the capital? That pairing is the mental model this lesson contributes; the full synthesis comes at the end of the module.

LESSON OBJECTIVE MET You should be able to state, without relying on any single figure, why LEO broadband became viable when it did: the three supply-side enablers, the three demand pillars, their convergence around 2020, and why GEO could not simply answer with more bandwidth. Carry the two-question test forward — it is the durable part.

eras = {

print(f"{'Era':<22}{'USD/kg to LEO':>16}")
print("-" * 38)
for name, cost in eras.items():
    print(f"{name:<22}{cost:>16,}")

def amortised_per_flight(build_cost, refurb_per_flight, flights):
    return build_cost / flights + refurb_per_flight

Exercise 3 — The convergence test

A toy model of "none of the three alone is sufficient": a system is viable only when satellite, launch, and terminal costs are all below threshold.

thresholds = {
    "sat_cost_usd":      2_000_000,
    "launch_usd_per_kg":     5_000,
    "terminal_usd":          1_000,
}

def is_viable(sat_cost, launch_per_kg, terminal):
    checks = {
        "satellite": sat_cost      <= thresholds["sat_cost_usd"],
        "launch":    launch_per_kg <= thresholds["launch_usd_per_kg"],
        "terminal":  terminal      <= thresholds["terminal_usd"],
    }
    return all(checks.values()), checks

eras = [
    ("1990s",       40_000_000, 18_000, 5_000),
    ("Early 2010s",  5_000_000, 10_000, 3_000),
    ("Early 2020s",    700_000,  2_700,   500),
]

for name, s, l, t in eras:
    viable, checks = is_viable(s, l, t)
    passed = [k for k, v in checks.items() if v]
    print(f"{name:<12} viable={viable}  passed: {passed}")

# 1. The early-2010s row passes some checks but not all. What does the
#    model imply for a system launched then?
# 2. The thresholds are invented. How would you ground them in the
#    break-even logic introduced later in the economics module?
EXTENSION Replace the hard thresholds with a single combined viability score and sweep one cost at a time to see which enabler the result is most sensitive to.
