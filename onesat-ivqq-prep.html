01 · Context — What OneSat Is

OneSat is Airbus DS's fully software-defined GEO communications satellite platform. Its differentiator is in-orbit reconfigurability: beam coverage, bandwidth allocation, and frequency plans can be changed post-launch via software upload, without hardware modification. Customers include Intelsat (Galaxy 35/36) and Eutelsat.

The Ground Segment for OneSat is consequently more complex than a traditional GEO ground system. It must manage dynamic payload configuration, TT&C for a software-defined payload, service management across reconfigurable beams, and the interfaces between the Network Management System, the Payload Control System, and the Mission Control System.

The IVVQ Manager owns validation end-to-end: from defining what "correct behaviour" looks like for a configurable system, to proving it at factory level and then on-site at the customer. The job is harder than for a fixed GEO payload because the validation space grows combinatorially with reconfiguration options.

04 · Likely Interview Questions — Prepared Answers
Walk us through an IVVQ strategy you defined from scratch. What drove your test environment choices?
Structure your answer around: (1) the system under test and what made it complex, (2) the validation objectives you derived from requirements, (3) the test environment trade — hardware-in-the-loop vs simulation vs stimulation — and why, (4) the phasing (unit/subsystem/system/acceptance), (5) the specific risk that drove each major decision. Close with a metric: how many anomalies were found in factory vs how many leaked to site acceptance or customer. Signal keywords: IVVQ plan · test coverage · HIL · stimulation · anomaly containment
OneSat payload is software-defined — coverage and frequency plans change via uplink. How does that affect your IVVQ approach?
The validation space grows combinatorially. Key points to hit: you can't test every configuration permutation, so the strategy must identify coverage equivalence classes and validate representatives. You need regression test suites that run after every configuration uplink. You must validate the configuration management process itself, not just the endpoint states. Simulation of the payload configuration interface (emulating the Payload Control System) becomes critical when hardware isn't available. Signal keywords: configuration equivalence · regression strategy · payload emulator · PCS interface
Describe a situation where you had to defend your IVVQ plan against schedule pressure from the program manager.
The panel wants to see that you hold your ground on validation integrity. Structure as: situation → what was being asked to skip or compress → your risk argument (not procedural, not rule-citing) → how you quantified the risk → the outcome. If you had a case where you were overruled and something did fail downstream, that's a stronger story than one where you always won. Show the reasoning, not just the result. Signal keywords: risk argument · test coverage delta · anomaly cost at customer
How do you set up and manage the test team? How do you handle resource conflicts across concurrent IVVQ projects?
G14 is senior IC, not head-of-department. The resource management question is real but scoped: you'll manage a specific IVVQ product team, not the whole division. Speak to: defining skillset requirements per test phase, working with line management for allocation, identifying when to use subcontractors vs internal, and keeping the team focused when CCB changes land mid-campaign. Mention test management tooling (HP ALM, Polarion, or equivalent) as your coordination mechanism. Signal keywords: resource planning · test campaign pacing · CCB impact · tool-driven coordination
What's your experience with Site Validation? What's different about it compared to Factory Integration Test?
Factory Integration Test: controlled environment, full instrumentation, engineer access to every interface. Site Validation: customer environment, limited access, real RF environment including interference, time pressure from contractual milestones, customer representatives watching. The delta: the ground station has its own existing infrastructure (antenna, SSPA, modem chain) that interacts with your delivered system. Anomalies at site are harder and more expensive to resolve. Your factory test must be designed to catch all issues you can catch in factory — Site Validation should be confirmation, not discovery. Signal keywords: FIT · SVT · delta environment · customer interface · anomaly containment by phase
Tell us about your participation in a CCB. How do you assess the IVVQ impact of a proposed change?
Your CCB role as IVVQ Manager is to classify proposed changes by their test impact: non-regression (existing test suite covers it), partial regression (targeted re-test needed), or full regression (campaign restart risk). You need to do this quickly and with a cost estimate. Describe your process: change description → affected functional chains → test cases that exercise those chains → campaign schedule impact → recommendation. If the change was imposed, document the delta and get sign-off. This is how you protect yourself contractually. Signal keywords: change impact classification · regression analysis · campaign delta · traceability
Why this role? Why now?
Keep it grounded. OneSat is the platform that defines ADS's next decade of commercial GEO. The ground segment for a software-defined satellite is a genuinely harder IVVQ problem than a fixed GEO equivalent. If you've been working on HBC+ ground segment architecture, the move to validation management of a next-generation platform is a clear and defensible step — deeper specialisation in the program that matters most to the division, rather than a lateral move. Do not over-pitch ambition. The panel wants a committed, stable IVVQ lead, not someone using this as a stepping stone.
